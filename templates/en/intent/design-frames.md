# Established Design Frames

This shared catalog helps compare established design frames that may fit a project. Blank sections in each scaffold are prompts for inquiry; do not present unresearched or unverified content as fact.

## id: persona

- name: Persona
- suitable situations: Use when a team needs a shared, representative view of users grounded in research about their behavior, goals, and difficulties to inform design decisions.
- draft scaffold: Use the following Markdown headings.
  - `### Audience and evidence`
  - `### Behaviors and context of use`
  - `### Goals and difficulties`
  - `### Assumptions to verify`
- misuse warning: Do not invent attributes from imagination or use one persona to represent every user. Separate research findings from assumptions that still need verification.
- source: Nielsen Norman Group, “Personas: Study Guide” (https://www.nngroup.com/articles/persona/)

## id: jobs-to-be-done

- name: Jobs to Be Done
- suitable situations: Use when the team needs to understand what progress a person seeks in a particular circumstance and examine alternatives around that goal instead of existing features or demographics.
- draft scaffold: Use the following Markdown headings.
  - `### User circumstance`
  - `### Job to be done`
  - `### Expected outcome`
  - `### Evidence and assumptions to verify`
- misuse warning: Do not state a solution or feature name as the job. Describe the circumstance and desired progress, supported by research evidence.
- source: Christensen Institute, “Jobs to Be Done Theory” (https://www.christenseninstitute.org/theory/jobs-to-be-done/)

## id: customer-journey-map

- name: Customer Journey Map
- suitable situations: Use when the team needs a time-oriented view of an experience toward one goal, including actions, thoughts, emotions, and difficulties across multiple touchpoints.
- draft scaffold: Use the following Markdown headings.
  - `### Actor and goal`
  - `### Stages and touchpoints`
  - `### Actions, thoughts, and emotions`
  - `### Difficulties and evidence`
  - `### Assumptions to verify`
- misuse warning: Do not map only an ideal successful path. Ground the map in research and include stalls, exits, and inconsistencies between touchpoints.
- source: Nielsen Norman Group, “Journey Mapping 101” (https://www.nngroup.com/articles/journey-mapping-101/)

## id: service-blueprint

- name: Service Blueprint
- suitable situations: Use when the team needs to relate user-visible touchpoints to employee activities, internal processes, and supporting resources that deliver the service.
- draft scaffold: Use the following Markdown headings.
  - `### Customer actions`
  - `### Frontstage touchpoints`
  - `### Backstage activities`
  - `### Supporting processes and resources`
  - `### Handoffs and failure points`
  - `### Evidence and assumptions to verify`
- misuse warning: Do not stop at the frontstage flow. Connect each touchpoint to backstage work, handoffs, waits, and failure points.
- source: Nielsen Norman Group, “Service Blueprints: Definition” (https://www.nngroup.com/articles/service-blueprints-definition/)

## id: user-story-mapping

- name: User Story Mapping
- suitable situations: Use when stakeholders need to arrange stories along the flow of user activities and discuss the whole experience and a coherent implementation scope.
- draft scaffold: Use the following Markdown headings.
  - `### User and goal`
  - `### Activity flow`
  - `### Stories for each activity`
  - `### Uncertainties and learning needs`
- misuse warning: Do not turn the map into a rearranged feature inventory. Use the user's activity flow as its backbone and revise details through conversation and learning.
- source: Jeff Patton & Associates, “Story Mapping” (https://jpattonassociates.com/story-mapping/)
